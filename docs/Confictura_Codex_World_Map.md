# Confictura Codex World Map

This is the map I would keep for myself while thinking about Confictura. It is
not a normal player guide, and it is not a system inventory. It is the shape of
the world as I understand it when I imagine a human player arriving with no
Confictura knowledge, maybe carrying old Ultima Online instincts, then slowly
learning that those instincts are only a starting grammar.

The human at the keyboard is always the center of this map. The character may
begin as a normal citizen, fugitive, savage, alien survivor, or creature-race
body, but the player is a person trying to understand a strange shard. My job
is to see the journey they are on: first confusion, then pattern recognition,
then self-definition, then permanence, then the long climb toward Ether.

## The Shape Of The World

Confictura is easiest to misunderstand if I treat it as Ultima Online with
extra scripts. It uses a RunUO skeleton, but the shard's own orientation says
it is not an era replica. It is a custom-systems freeshard with a multi-map
world, unusual origins, alternate skill caps, creature-race play, many magic
and class fantasies, dynamic encounters, champion and invasion events,
player-run cities, homestead production, boats, XML-driven world tools, and a
canonical character-level layer. ?F:docs/wiki/Confictura_Introduction.md†L16-L20??

That tells me the first rule: old UO knowledge helps with verbs, not meaning.
Double-click, backpack, bank, paperdoll, skill gain, guards, boats, books,
magic, combat, and housing all still look familiar enough to invite habits.
But the world is not Britannia as a fixed cultural object. It is Confictura:
an adventure sandbox where the player chooses a fate, travels through multiple
worlds, grows through skills and identity, faces encounters that care about
region and level, and can build civic or domestic systems of their own.
?F:docs/wiki/Confictura_Introduction.md†L22-L33??

So my mental map has layers rather than zones:

- The immediate layer: controls, survival, status, bank, backpack, death, and
  chat.
- The fate layer: where the character begins, what society thinks of them, and
  what cap family they inherit.
- The identity layer: which skills, magic schools, weapons, professions, and
  reputations become their real class.
- The world-pressure layer: encounters, dungeons, invasions, champions, AI,
  monsters, and the risk of being noticed.
- The permanence layer: homes, cities, boats, farms, books, vendors, guilds,
  player economy, and civic power.
- The ascent layer: character level, reputation, cap expansion, Ether, and the
  feeling that the character has become more than a local adventurer.

The map names matter because they are a clue that lore has moved. Confictura's
worlds include Lodor, Sosaria, Underworld, Serpent Island, Isles Dread,
Savaged Empire, Atlantis, and Internal in the shard's current world identity.
?F:docs/wiki/Confictura_Introduction.md†L35-L47?? A player may recognize some
words, but recognition is not mastery. Each map is a rule surface and a mood,
not just a facet label.

## The First Human Hour

The first human hour should feel like waking up inside a familiar interface
that refuses to be the familiar game. The player knows enough to move, open a
pack, inspect skills, talk to NPCs, and die. What they do not know is what any
of that means here.

The Guide to Adventure tells me what the shard expects a new player to learn:
status, chat, banks, inns, practice, combat, weapons, armor, gates, runes,
books of magic, death, and finally the practical advice to explore, ask sages,
find resource or skill instruction, loot dungeons, handle traps, and carry
reagents, food, and drink. ?F:docs/wiki/Guide_To_Adventure_Book.md†L73-L87??
That sequence is not just help text. It is the first cognitive ladder.

From the player's perspective, the first hour has several questions:

- Where am I?
- What can kill me?
- What did my starting choice do to me?
- Which commands matter?
- Can other players hurt me?
- How do I recover from mistakes?
- What should I become?

PvP consent is part of that first-hour safety model. The system stores a
player's preference as Neutral, PvP, PvE, or temporary event state, and the
choice gump explains that PvP players can fight other eligible players while
PvE players cannot attack players and cannot perform beneficial actions on PvP
players. ?F:docs/wiki/PvP_Consent_System.md†L24-L31??
?F:docs/wiki/PvP_Consent_System.md†L41-L49?? This matters because Confictura
does not ask a new human to understand the whole social order before they know
how to walk. It offers a social risk posture early.

My internal model of onboarding is therefore not "teach every system." It is
"give the player enough handles to continue forming questions." The first hour
does not need to explain Titan, government, homestead, random encounter
aliases, or every magic school. It needs to make the player believe that
Confictura is learnable, that death is recoverable, that their chosen path
matters, and that the world is larger than the first town.

## Choosing A Fate

The fate layer is where Confictura most clearly breaks from old UO assumptions.
The tarot greeter is not merely a spawn menu. The existing orientation says
character creation offers a fate, and several routes deliberately change the
character model. ?F:docs/wiki/Confictura_Introduction.md†L55-L60??

The ordinary human starts should feel like recognizable civilization. They put
the player into named places with enough cultural signal to begin asking local
questions. The fugitive route is different: it begins in the Britain Dungeons,
makes guards hostile, restricts civilized life, doubles resurrection costs,
narrows guild access, and grants a larger skill-cap path in exchange for social
danger. ?F:docs/wiki/Confictura_Introduction.md†L62-L67?? That is not a class.
It is a criminal cosmology. The player has more room to grow because the world
pushes back harder.

The savage route is another social contract. It starts in the Savaged Empire
and frames the character as barbaric and survival-first, with extra skill
capacity, harsh wilderness, dinosaurs, dangerous hunting, and survival gear.
?F:docs/wiki/Confictura_Introduction.md†L69-L74?? The player is not simply
"starting elsewhere." They are accepting a different relationship to comfort,
law, equipment, and learning.

The alien route is the strongest warning that Confictura is not bound by UO
lore genre. It begins at a shuttle crash site, with stasis, no memory, no
skills, no gold, a huge learning ceiling, trouble with magical resurrection,
and no luck benefits. ?F:docs/wiki/Confictura_Introduction.md†L76-L80?? In my
map, the alien is the purest beginner. The player knows nothing; the character
knows nothing; the world itself is partly incomprehensible. Its huge cap is not
just generosity. It is the promise that blankness can become scope.

Creature-race starts complicate the phrase "all humans." The player is human,
but the shard can put that human consciousness into a very different body and
starting ecology. Race starts can choose cave, ice, pits, sand, sea, sky,
swamp, tomb, water, or woods, and the race system maps those to readable zone
names such as The Cave, The Tundra, The Desert, The Sea, The Mountains, and The
Woods. ?F:docs/wiki/Confictura_Introduction.md†L84-L89?? I should treat these
as embodied introductions: the player learns the world first through habitat.

The important design reading is this: Confictura's start is not "choose what
you are good at." It is "choose what kind of problem the world will be for you."

## When The World Clicks

The click happens when the player stops asking "what is different from UO?" and
starts asking "what is my path through Confictura?" That shift happens through
skills, archetypes, reputation, travel, and pressure.

Character level is one of the key unifiers. For player mobiles, the live level
formula converts normalized power into a 1-100 level, and power blends skill
power, stat progress, and reputation progress. ?F:docs/wiki/Character_Level_Recon_Report.md†L13-L28??
The best adventure power comes from tracked adventure archetypes such as
martial, archer, assassin, ninja, samurai, knight, death knight, arcane mage,
elementalist, necromancer, witch, druid, holy man, mystic monk, Jedi, Syth,
researcher, ranger, bard, thief, and jester. ?F:docs/wiki/Character_Level_Recon_Report.md†L30-L54??

That means the shard does not only reward total skill accumulation. It tries to
recognize who the character is becoming. A fighter becomes legible as a
fighter. A bard becomes legible as a bard. A dark spiritual build becomes
legible differently from a holy one. A thief is not just a failed warrior. The
player can start to see that Confictura is measuring shape, not only mass.

Random encounters reinforce this. Eligible connected player characters are
selected by region category, with staff, dead players, and hidden players
handled through specific rules. Encounter lookup uses map, region name, time
bucket, and land bucket before choosing an encounter bucket. ?F:docs/wiki/Random_Encounter_Engine.md†L46-L74??
Level gates then check encounters against the character-level service for
players. ?F:docs/wiki/Random_Encounter_Engine.md†L76-L80??

This is where the world starts feeling aware. Dungeons are not just static
rooms. Caves, bard dungeons, outdoor regions, villages, map names, land, time,
and level all become part of the pressure field. A player who has only played
stock UO might expect danger to be mostly placed content. Confictura also has
placed content, but the more important feeling is that wandering itself has
systems watching it.

The magic and archetype spread is the other click. Confictura's identity is not
limited to warrior, mage, bard, tamer. The world supports knights, death
knights, elementalists, necromancers, witches, druids, holy men, mystics, Jedi,
Syth, researchers, rangers, jesters, crafters, gatherers, merchants, seafarers,
aliens, and creature races through documentation and level-service lanes.
?F:docs/wiki/Confictura_Introduction.md†L137-L166?? To me, this is the point
where the player starts thinking in destinies rather than templates.

## Becoming Permanent

Early play is about orientation. Midgame is about identity. Permanence is about
leaving a mark that remains when the player logs out.

Government is the clearest permanence system. A mayor can place a city hall
deed, and a placed city hall creates the city region, management stone, voting
stone, and initial mayor/citizen state. ?F:docs/wiki/Government_System.md†L3-L12??
Founding a city has placement restrictions, starts with treasury, timers, a
default name, one citizen, and one vote stone, and city level later depends on
citizen count. ?F:docs/wiki/Government_System.md†L14-L37?? Cities can disband
if they fall below six citizens or lose valid leadership, and membership
depends on player housing within the city. ?F:docs/wiki/Government_System.md†L39-L42??

That means civic power in Confictura is not decoration. It is a population,
housing, treasury, election, region, and service machine. To a player, a city
is the moment the world stops being only something they consume.

Homestead systems give a quieter version of the same permanence. The package
includes crop planting and harvesting, fruit trees, cooking, brewing, juicing,
winecrafting, a community beehive, and milk-and-cheese systems. ?F:docs/wiki/Homestead_System.md†L3-L5??
Seeds must be carried, planting depends on tile and crowding rules, growth
uses timers, and success can depend on the sower's Cooking skill. ?F:docs/wiki/Homestead_System.md†L6-L16??
This is permanence at human scale: a player comes back because something is
growing, fermenting, refilling, producing, or waiting to be harvested.

Boats turn permanence into mobility. Launching a boat requires the item in the
backpack, valid region and placement checks, and usually dock proximity for
non-carpet boats. On success, the boat records ownership, starts anchored,
creates bank and backpack keys, hues components, and can reveal deeper boat
features for strong Seafaring characters. ?F:docs/wiki/Boat_Core_Mechanics.md†L21-L38??
In my map, boats are not just travel devices. They are portable bases, cargo
decisions, command surfaces, and a declaration that the player now thinks in
routes.

Economy, books, vendors, bulk orders, relics, grave robbing, librarian work,
runic crafting, taxidermy, and trade systems live around these loops. I do not
need every one of them in the foreground at all times. I need to remember that
Confictura's long-term player is not only a combatant. They may be an author,
farmer, brewer, mayor, sailor, hunter, artifact collector, criminal, merchant,
or civic organizer.

## Toward Titan And Ether

The Titan/Ether path should not be understood as "more cap" alone. It is the
second ascent of a character whose identity has already become visible.

The live cap model is explicit. Default, Savage, Fugitive, and Alien starts
have different skill-start families, and the system prefers the character's
actual `Skills.Cap` while falling back to `SkillStart + SkillBoost +
SkillEther` when needed. ?F:docs/wiki/Character_Level_Recon_Report.md†L56-L72??
The Titan/Ether reward path adds 5000 to `Skills.Cap`, sets `SkillEther =
5000`, and raises `StatCap` to 300. ?F:docs/wiki/Character_Level_Recon_Report.md†L73-L74??

For me, Ether is the place where Confictura's progression language becomes
mythic. The player has already learned controls, survived a fate, built a
class identity, read the world's pressure, found allies or territory, and
understood that the game sees more than raw skill total. Ether says: now widen
the vessel.

That is why the ascent should be framed carefully. A titan of Ether is not just
a character with more numbers. It is a character who can carry more of the
world's verbs without losing identity. The default adventurer may become a
master. The fugitive may become a feared legend. The savage may become a
wilderness sovereign. The alien may become an impossible generalist. The
creature-race character may become the proof that a human player can inhabit a
different ecology and still make meaning.

The final shape of the journey, as I see it, is:

1. The human logs in and recognizes the interface.
2. The world refuses to be the old world.
3. The player chooses a fate and receives a problem.
4. The player learns survival verbs.
5. The player discovers that systems are watching identity.
6. The player focuses into an archetype.
7. The world begins responding with appropriate pressure.
8. The player makes something permanent.
9. The character widens through cap, reputation, and mastery.
10. Ether turns mastery into scope.
